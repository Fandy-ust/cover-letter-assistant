# Style Guidelines

## Tone & Voice
- Lead with capability and outputs (projects, research, systems), not constraints or “status” details.
- Keep it confident and direct, but not aggressive; let the evidence (links, results, concrete work) do the persuading.

## Sentence Structure
- Short, skimmable sentences. One idea per sentence.
- Prefer bullets for the core argument; each bullet should have a crisp bold lead-in.

## Vocabulary Preferences
- Natural, concrete phrasing over formal “application-speak”.
- Use “what I built / what I work on / what I shipped / what I tested” language where true.

## Opening & Closing Patterns
- Opening should quickly state the role and then immediately introduce what you work on (your strongest projects/research themes).
- Avoid putting “Year X” or eligibility constraints in the first sentence unless it’s beneficial; those details can be addressed later.
- Closing stays simple and polite.

## Structure & Formatting
- Preferred flow: 1–2 line intro → bullets highlighting 2–4 strongest capabilities/projects → end with a short “fit + availability” bullet (languages/location/constraints).
- If there’s a potential concern (e.g., “Year 3+” requirement), address it briefly near the end after you’ve established ability.

## Unique Quirks
- Include direct links (arXiv, GitHub, portfolio) inline where relevant, but keep them readable (no link spam).
